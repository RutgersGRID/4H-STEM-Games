Introduction to Game Design

Designing a game is both an art and a science. Having a design document as a foundation can help organize your ideas and guide you or your team through development. Here are some activities to get you started.

Try using the "Design the Box" and "Paper Prototype" activities to bring core ideas to the surface, which you can then use to solidify your Game Design Document. Or... do something completely unrelated! Inspiration can strike when you least expect it. The most important thing is to write it down somewhere!

Activity 1: Design the Box!

Imagine your game is already made and sitting on a store shelf. What's on the cover? What do you want people to notice? What does the art tell you about the tone of the gameâ€”is it serious or whimsical? Who are you trying to reach? Now, look at the back of the box. What features do you want to highlight? The story? The gameplay (the "verbs" or actions)? What screenshots would you include?

Activity 2: Paper Prototyping

The fastest way to test your idea is to prototype. Instead of jumping into code, spending hours drawing perfect characters, or writing the perfect document only to find out the game isn't fun, paper prototype! Make it quick and ugly. The more you iterate, the quicker you improve.

Use sticky notes for each character, place, action, story beat, or item. Shuffle them. Group them. Line them up to tell a linear story or branch them out like a tree. Move them around like pieces in a board game. Play the game. Was it fun? Didn't like it? Iterate! Use dice to add random elements. Find the fun, and when something works, capture it in your design document!

Activity 3: Research (aka Playing Games)

As a designer, you should play games critically. Dissect their parts and analyze them. To do this effectively, try keeping a player journal. While you play, jot down notes answering the questions below. This will help you build your design vocabulary and knowledge to use when creating your next game.

  • What are the main verbs (actions the player can take)?
  • Who is the intended audience for this game?
  • How does the environment tell a story?
  • What are the core game mechanics?
  • How does the game provide feedback when you're doing something right (or wrong)?
  • What don't I like about this game, and why?
  • What is this game trying to make me feel or think? How does it achieve that?
  • How are the characters represented?

Applying Game Mechanics to Education

How can we make learning more engaging, immersive, and fun? By applying the same mechanics that make games so compelling! Things like clear goals, immediate feedback, a sense of progression, and storytelling can transform a standard lesson into an unforgettable experience.
